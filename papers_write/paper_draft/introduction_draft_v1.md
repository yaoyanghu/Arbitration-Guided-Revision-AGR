# Introduction Draft V1

Evidence retrieval quality remains a central bottleneck in fact verification pipelines. Even when candidate generation is strong enough to place relevant evidence somewhere in the retrieved set, downstream performance still depends heavily on whether the correct evidence page appears near the top of the ranking. This motivates a narrower retrieval-stage question: can lightweight reranking improve evidence ordering inside a fixed lexical candidate pool without requiring a more complex retrieval stack?

This paper studies that question on official FEVER. Our scope is deliberately limited. We focus on evidence retrieval rather than end-to-end claim verification, and we do not present this work as a complete ChronoRAG system. Instead, we study lightweight title-aware lexical reranking on top of a BM25 candidate retriever and evaluate whether such reranking improves strict evidence-page ranking. The focus on strict evaluation matters because the central retrieval problem here is not only whether related pages appear somewhere in the candidate set, but whether the official gold evidence page is promoted to the top of the ranking.

Our main independent validation anchor is a disjoint 1000-query official FEVER split constructed to avoid overlap with the earlier tuning subset. On this split, the BM25 baseline achieves strict Recall@1 / Recall@5 / Recall@10 of 0.368 / 0.627 / 0.720. A token-level title-overlap reranker improves these values to 0.415 / 0.680 / 0.720. The unchanged Recall@10 indicates that the improvement arises from reranking within an already retrieved candidate pool rather than from expanded candidate coverage. This is the core empirical result of the paper.

However, the strongest defensible paper claim is broader than the title-overlap variant alone. In addition to the main BM25 vs title-overlap comparison, we evaluate a nearest simpler title-aware lexical baseline based on exact page-title boosting. That baseline is stronger than token-level title overlap on the same disjoint split. Accordingly, the paper should not claim that title overlap is the uniquely validated best method. The correct claim is narrower and more robust: lightweight title-aware lexical reranking improves official FEVER evidence retrieval, while title overlap is one conservative variant within that broader family.

Within this scope, the paper makes four contributions. First, it isolates a retrieval-stage problem on official FEVER: improving strict gold-page ranking inside a fixed BM25 candidate pool. Second, it shows that a title-overlap reranker yields statistically supported improvements in early-rank strict retrieval quality over BM25. Third, it strengthens the experimental package with strict-versus-relaxed evaluation, label-wise analysis, case analysis, and paired significance with bootstrap confidence intervals. Fourth, it situates the title-overlap variant against a nearest title-aware lexical baseline, showing that exact title boost is stronger among the tested lightweight heuristics and thereby sharpening the level at which the paper's claim should be made.
